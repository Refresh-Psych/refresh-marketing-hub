SEGMENT 1: The Nushama Story — From Pandemic Launch to Emerging Treatment Leader

To understand why Nushama matters so much as a case study, you have to understand what the landscape looked like when they launched in 2020. Ketamine therapy for depression had been around as an off-label treatment since the early 2000s. The FDA had approved esketamine — the nasal spray version, marketed as Spravato — in 2019. But the psychiatric establishment was still deeply cautious. Most psychiatrists were watching from the sidelines, waiting for more evidence, more insurance coverage, more certainty before they committed.

Nushama's founders — Dina Shulman and Jay Godfrey, a business executive and a fashion designer respectively — were not psychiatrists at all. They were entrepreneurs who had personally experienced the life-changing effects of ketamine therapy for their own mental health challenges. They saw a gaping market need: people with treatment-resistant depression, PTSD, and anxiety who had failed multiple medication trials and were desperate for something different. And they saw a clinical modality with extraordinary evidence behind it that most practices were too cautious to offer.

So they built a luxury ketamine clinic in New York City. Not the sterile medical office most people imagined. A spa-like environment. Carefully designed lighting. Specially curated playlists. Integration therapy sessions before and after ketamine treatments. Their model was: ketamine is not just a drug infusion — it's a therapeutic journey, and the set and setting are as important as the medicine itself.

Here's where it gets interesting from a business perspective. They had three major strategic insights that most practices missed completely.

First insight: they positioned ketamine NOT as a last resort for the sickest patients, but as an advanced first-line alternative for patients who were suffering and didn't want to spend years on the medication trial-and-error treadmill. This was controversial in 2020. Most psychiatrists were still recommending ketamine only for treatment-resistant depression — patients who had failed three or more medication trials. Nushama said: why should patients have to fail three times before they access a potentially transformative treatment? Their messaging was: if you've been struggling and traditional medications haven't worked fast enough or well enough, there is a faster path. That positioning expanded their potential patient base enormously.

Second insight: they invested heavily in the experience layer around ketamine, not just the ketamine itself. The research literature was clear that ketamine works through neuroplasticity — it literally rewires connections in the brain, particularly in the cortico-limbic circuits involved in depression and emotional regulation. But they understood that the quality of the experience during and after the treatment mattered for long-term outcomes. A 2026 study from their own practice actually documented structural changes in the default mode network after a single ketamine session. They positioned themselves as researchers, not just clinicians — publishing case series, contributing to literature, speaking at conferences. That gave them credibility that traditional ketamine clinics didn't have.

Third insight — and this is the one that every practice owner should write down — they built their content marketing engine before they had significant patient volume. Their blog, their Instagram, their educational videos about the science of ketamine were running from day one. They weren't just treating patients; they were educating the public about emerging treatments, building organic search authority on terms like "ketamine for depression," "treatment-resistant depression options," and "how does ketamine therapy work." By the time they reached scale, they were ranking on the first page of Google for dozens of high-intent search terms in their market. The patients were finding THEM.

By 2023, Nushama had expanded to multiple New York locations and begun developing their partnership model with traditional psychiatric practices that wanted to add emerging treatments without building the infrastructure from scratch. That partnership model is directly relevant to what Refresh Psychiatry could do with its 16-location network.

Now here's the data that should make every practice owner pay attention. As of early 2026, there are over 2,800 Spravato-certified clinics in the United States. Prescriptions for esketamine have nearly doubled since 2023. The FDA approved Spravato as a monotherapy — not just an adjunct to oral antidepressants — in January 2025. That means the prior oral antidepressant requirement was eliminated, expanding the eligible patient pool dramatically. And the clinical trial data for psilocybin therapy is so strong that Compass Pathways is expected to file an NDA for their psilocybin compound COMP360 in Q4 of 2026.

Emerging treatments are no longer emerging. They are arriving. The question for practice owners is not "if" but "when" and "how."

SEGMENT 2: What Nushama Got Right, What Nearly Broke Them, and the Lessons for Multi-Location Practices

Let me tell you about the part of Nushama's story that doesn't make it into the press releases.

The first two years were financially brutal. Building a high-quality ketamine clinic is expensive. The infusion equipment, the medical-grade recliners, the integration therapy staff, the pharmacy partnerships, the REMS certification for Spravato — all of this required significant capital before a single patient walked through the door. Early projections underestimated how long it would take to educate the market and build a referral network from scratch. They were running at a significant loss through most of 2021 and into 2022.

The mistake they almost made — and that many practices DO make — was building out too much space too fast. Their original New York build-out was designed for a capacity they didn't reach for almost two years. If you're thinking about adding Spravato or TMS to your practice, the financial discipline lesson from Nushama is: start small, prove the model in one location, then replicate. Do not build a six-chair infusion suite when you're projecting three patients per week.

The second challenge was the referral network problem. Nushama was not working within a traditional psychiatry practice — they were a standalone ketamine clinic. That meant they needed to build relationships with psychiatrists, therapists, and PCPs who would refer their most difficult patients. In the early days, the psychiatry establishment was skeptical. Ketamine for depression felt edgy. Some practitioners were openly dismissive. The founders had to spend enormous time educating referral partners about the clinical evidence, the safety profile, and the monitoring protocols. It took two full years before they had a reliable referral pipeline.

Here's why this is actually GREAT news for Refresh Psychiatry and similar practices that are already established psychiatric providers. You do not have this problem. You have patient panels across 16 locations. You have insurance credentials. You have established provider relationships. You have brand recognition. Adding emerging treatment options to an existing practice has a fundamentally better economics profile than launching a standalone clinic from scratch.

Let's talk about the TMS story, because it's a different but equally instructive case. A practice in the Tampa Bay area — this was documented in a Psychiatric Times case report — added a BrainsWay Deep TMS system to their outpatient psychiatric practice in 2022. Their psychiatrist had the same hesitations most practitioners have: Will insurance cover it? How do we staff the daily treatments? Will patients actually complete the 30-plus session protocol? The answers: yes, progressively better; with a part-time technician; and yes, once patients start feeling better around sessions 10 to 15.

Their first year with TMS was modest — about 15 patients completed full courses. By year two, they had insurance preauthorizations down to a science, they were running two chairs with a dedicated technician, and they were seeing 35 to 40 patients per year through TMS courses. At approximately $12,000 per treatment course after insurance adjustments, that's $420,000 to $480,000 in additional revenue from a $150,000 equipment investment. Full return on investment achieved in year two, with years three and four being largely pure margin.

That's the TMS math. And the math improved again in November 2025 when BrainsWay received FDA clearance for Deep TMS in adolescents ages 15 to 21. That is a historically underserved population with very few evidence-based treatment options and high demand.

Now let me push back on something here, because I want to be balanced. Not every emerging treatment has these economics. Let me tell you about where practices go wrong.

IV ketamine infusion suites — as opposed to Spravato, which is the FDA-approved nasal spray — have a more complex risk profile for a telepsychiatry practice like Refresh. IV ketamine requires an anesthesiologist or CRNA monitoring, a designated infusion room, very specific equipment, and the logistical complexity of managing a Schedule II controlled substance infusion. The Spravato program, by contrast, is manageable in an outpatient psychiatric office. Patients self-administer the nasal spray under the provider's observation. A two-hour monitoring period is required. But you can run three to four Spravato sessions per day per room. The infrastructure investment is dramatically lower than IV ketamine.

Similarly, the psychedelic-assisted therapy space — MDMA and psilocybin — is still in regulatory limbo as of March 2026. MDMA-assisted therapy for PTSD was rejected by the FDA in 2024 and is under re-review. Psilocybin is still a Schedule I substance at the federal level even as Compass Pathways advances their NDA. Oregon and Colorado have state-level frameworks, but federally supervised practice integration is not imminent. For a multi-state practice like Refresh that operates across Florida, Massachusetts, and Texas, psychedelics-related services remain a 2028-plus conversation — not a 2026 action item.

The practical takeaway: emerging treatments require differentiated business planning. Spravato is actionable NOW. TMS is actionable NOW. IV ketamine and digital therapeutics require careful evaluation. Psychedelics are a horizon play.

SEGMENT 3: The Refresh Psychiatry Emerging Treatment Roadmap — What to Do in the Next 90 Days

Let me give you the specific, actionable playbook for a 16-location telepsychiatry practice looking to add emerging treatments without disrupting your core medication management business.

Step one. Get REMS certified for Spravato at your highest-volume Florida location. The esketamine REMS certification is a provider-level certification that any licensed prescriber can obtain. It requires completing an online training module — typically two to four hours. Once certified, your practice can prescribe Spravato and supervise administration sessions. The requirement is that the patient self-administers in your office and stays for a two-hour monitoring period. For Refresh, the Davie location would be the logical first site. You already have the patient volume and the infrastructure.

The business case: Spravato for treatment-resistant depression bills at approximately $800 to $1,200 per session through commercial insurance. Patients typically receive two sessions per week for four weeks in the acute phase, then maintenance sessions monthly. That's eight to sixteen thousand dollars in the acute treatment phase alone. Aetna, United, Cigna, and Humana all have coverage policies for Spravato in treatment-resistant depression. You are already credentialed with all of these payers.

Step two. Build the patient identification protocol. Work with your providers to systematically identify patients who meet the Spravato criteria: adults with treatment-resistant depression who have failed at least two oral antidepressants. With 16 locations and an established patient panel, Refresh almost certainly has hundreds of patients who qualify. A simple note in your EHR flagging treatment-resistant patients and introducing the Spravato option could generate significant new revenue while serving patients who are not achieving adequate relief with current treatments.

Step three. Develop the content narrative. Nushama didn't just add emerging treatments — they told a story about them. For Refresh, the story is: we are a practice at the frontier of psychiatric science. We don't just prescribe the same medications that have been used for thirty years. We offer the most advanced treatment options including DNA-guided prescribing through pharmacogenomics and next-generation treatments like esketamine for patients who haven't found relief elsewhere. That narrative differentiates Refresh from every Talkiatry and Cerebral competitor that is running the same medication management assembly line.

Step four. Evaluate the TMS opportunity for your highest-TRD-volume locations. TMS is a larger infrastructure investment than Spravato — equipment costs range from $80,000 to $150,000, or you can lease TMS systems for $5,000 to $10,000 per month. The insurance coverage landscape is strong: Medicare and most commercial payers cover TMS for MDD that has failed one or more antidepressants. The newly cleared adolescent indication expands your potential pool significantly. The BrainsWay Deep TMS system, now cleared for patients 15 and older, is particularly relevant for Refresh given your multi-location footprint in South Florida where adolescent mental health demand is acute.

Step five. Subscribe to the psilocybin trial database. You cannot administer psilocybin yet, but your patients are asking about it. Fifty-seven percent of psychiatrists surveyed in 2025 reported patients asking about psychedelic therapy. The Compass Pathways COMP360 NDA is expected in Q4 2026. When that approval comes, the practices that have educated their patient population and built their clinical team's knowledge base will be positioned to add the service immediately. The practices that haven't done the groundwork will spend twelve to eighteen months catching up.
