1. 💊 FDA Drug Approvals & Safety Alerts

▼
🆕 Bysanti (milsaperidone) — Approved February 20, 2026
FDA approved Bysanti tablets for acute manic/mixed episodes in bipolar I disorder and schizophrenia in adults. This is a new chemical entity (NCE) — an active metabolite of iloperidone targeting 5-HT2A and D2 receptors. Commercial availability expected Q3 2026. Vanda Pharmaceuticals is also running a Phase III trial for adjunctive treatment of treatment-resistant MDD.
🆕 New Approval

Practice Impact: Once available in Q3, Bysanti may offer an alternative atypical antipsychotic with potentially improved tolerability vs. iloperidone. Monitor launch pricing and payer coverage. Consider for patients with akathisia concerns on current antipsychotics.

🆕 ProlivRx (Neurolief) — FDA Approved Neuromodulation Device
First prescription, physician-directed, at-home brain neuromodulation therapy approved as adjunctive treatment for adults with MDD who failed ≥1 antidepressant. Non-invasive, home-based.
🆕 New Approval

Practice Impact: Could be a valuable add-on for treatment-resistant patients managed via telehealth. Investigate prescribing workflow and insurance coverage for Refresh's patient population.
🔄 Cobenfy (KarXT) — ARISE Phase 3 Misses Endpoint
Bristol-Myers Squibb's Cobenfy (xanomeline/trospium) failed to show statistically significant difference vs. placebo as adjunctive treatment for inadequately controlled schizophrenia in the ARISE study. The drug remains approved for monotherapy in schizophrenia.
🔄 Developing

Practice Impact: Cobenfy's role may be limited to monotherapy or switching. Do not yet rely on it as an augmentation strategy for partially responsive patients.
🆕 Esketamine (Spravato) — Now Approved as Monotherapy for TRD
Previously approved only as adjunctive therapy, Spravato received expanded approval as a standalone treatment for adults with treatment-resistant depression.
🆕 Label Expansion

Practice Impact: Significant for Refresh — simplifies Spravato protocols by removing requirement for concurrent oral antidepressant. Review referral partnerships with Spravato-certified centers near your FL, MA, and TX locations.

2. 📋 Clinical Practice Guideline Updates

▼

🔄 APA Telehealth Practice Guidelines — February 2026 Update

The APA published updated guidance addressing the February 2026 Medicare telehealth changes, clarifying in-person visit requirements for new vs. established patients in behavioral health telehealth. Key points:

  • New Medicare patients must have an in-person visit within 6 months of initiating telehealth
  • Established patients require in-person visit at least once every 12 months
  • Audio-only for mental/behavioral health remains permanently allowed
  • Patients who began telehealth behavioral health services on or before Jan 30, 2026 are grandfathered as "established"

Refresh Action: Audit your Medicare patient roster to identify any patients needing an in-person visit. Consider partnering with local PCP offices near each location for required in-person touchpoints.

🔗 APA: Critical Telehealth Changes

📌 42 CFR Part 2 — Full Enforcement Begins February 16, 2026

Updated federal regulations governing confidentiality of substance use disorder (SUD) patient records are now in full enforcement. Key changes align Part 2 protections more closely with HIPAA, but still require specific patient consent for SUD records sharing in most cases.

Refresh Action: Ensure all EHR consent forms and BAAs reflect updated Part 2 requirements, especially for patients receiving buprenorphine or other SUD treatments via telehealth.

6. 📜 DEA & Controlled Substance Policy Changes

▼
✅ Telehealth Controlled Substance Prescribing Extended Through Dec 31, 2026
The DEA and HHS issued the Fourth Temporary Extension of COVID-19 telemedicine flexibilities. All DEA-registered providers can continue prescribing Schedule II–V controlled substances via telehealth without an in-person evaluation through end of 2026.

Key Details:
  • Applies to ALL practitioner-patient relationships regardless of when established
  • Audio-only permitted for Schedule III–V narcotics (OUD treatment)
  • No new documentation, registration, or technology requirements imposed
  • 7+ million telehealth controlled substance Rx were written in 2024 alone
Action Required? No — maintain current workflows but prepare for potential permanent rules in 2027.
🔗 DEA Press Release | HHS Announcement
⚠️ Permanent Telehealth Prescribing Rules — Still Pending
The Biden-era proposed rule for Special Registrations for Telemedicine remains un-finalized. The Trump administration has not moved forward. The proposed rule would create special DEA registrations with requirements for PDMP checks, audio-video technology, Schedule II restrictions, data reporting, identity verification, and record retention.

Action Required? Monitor — Track the rulemaking process. Final regulations expected before Dec 31, 2026 deadline. Begin building compliance readiness.
🔗 Federal Register Notice

💡 Strategic Implications for Refresh Psychiatry

The Big Picture

The 2026 telehealth landscape is best described as "extended but uncertain." Current flexibilities are in place through year-end, but permanent rules remain unfinished. Here's what this means strategically:

1. Short-Term (Now–Dec 2026): Business as usual, with preparation. Continue telehealth-first operations. Current DEA/Medicare flexibilities protect the model. Use this window to strengthen operational compliance (PDMP checks, documentation standards, identity verification) so you're ahead when permanent rules drop.

2. Medium-Term (2027): The transition year. Permanent DEA telehealth rules will likely take effect. Medicare flexibilities extend through end of 2027. Begin planning for potential in-person requirements — evaluate lightweight physical touchpoints (partner clinics, coworking medical spaces) near patient clusters in each market.

3. Long-Term (2028+): Hybrid is the future. Pure-play telehealth may face regulatory headwinds. The practices that thrive will offer a "telehealth-first, in-person-when-needed" model. Refresh's 16 physical locations are actually a strategic advantage over pure-play competitors like Cerebral or Done — lean into this.

4. Licensing strategy: Leverage IMLC for efficient multi-state expansion. Monitor the Social Work Compact launch for therapy staffing flexibility across FL/MA/TX.

5. Advocacy: Engage with Florida Psychiatric Society (Spring 2026 meeting), Massachusetts Medical Society, and Texas Medical Association to advocate for permanent telehealth flexibilities.
